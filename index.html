Frequently Asked Questions

What is password entropy and why does it matter?

Password entropy measures unpredictability in bits. Higher entropy means more possible combinations an attacker must try. A password with 40 bits of entropy has 240 (roughly 1 trillion) possible combinations. For strong security, aim for at least 60–80 bits. The formula is: E = L × log₂(C), where L is length and C is the character set size.

What is the difference between MD5, SHA-1, SHA-256, and bcrypt?

MD5 produces a 128-bit hash and is cryptographically broken — never use for security. SHA-1 (160-bit) is deprecated due to collision attacks. SHA-256 (256-bit, SHA-2 family) is currently secure for most applications. bcrypt is a password-hashing function with built-in salt and configurable cost factor, making it intentionally slow and resistant to brute-force — recommended for storing passwords.

How does the Data Breach Scanner work?

The scanner checks whether an email or domain has appeared in known data breaches. It queries publicly available breach databases using privacy-first methods such as k-anonymity — meaning your full email is never transmitted. The tool does not store, log, or share any queried data.

Is my data safe when using this tool?

Yes. The Cryptographic Command Center uses a zero-knowledge architecture. Passwords and text entered into the entropy analyzer and hash engine are processed entirely within your browser — nothing is transmitted to any server. No data is stored, logged, or shared. The breach scanner uses k-anonymity when querying external databases.

What is crack time estimation?

Crack time estimates how long an attacker would need to brute-force your password. Offline fast hash assumes access to unsalted MD5/SHA-1 hashes at ~10 billion guesses/sec. Online throttled assumes a rate-limited web form at ~100 guesses/sec. These are theoretical — actual times vary by hardware and method.
